How trauma lives in the body

How Trauma Affects the Nervous System

Many people believe that if they understand their experiences intellectually, they should be able to move forward. But trauma is not stored only in thoughts; it is stored in the nervous system.

That is why your logical mind might know that what happened is over, and yet your body continues to respond as if it is not.

Trauma as a body experience

When something overwhelming happens, the nervous system may remain stuck in survival responses such as fight, flight, or shutdown — even years later.

When the body experiences something overwhelming, it can remain stuck in survival responses such as fight, flight, or shutdown. Even years later, the nervous system may continue reacting as if danger is still present.

This can show up as:

  • anxiety or hypervigilance
  • emotional overwhelm
  • numbness or disconnection
  • difficulty relaxing
  • feeling constantly on edge

Trauma therapy focuses on helping the nervous system settle so that healing can occur.

EMDR therapy

EMDR (Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing) is a well-researched trauma therapy used to help the brain process unresolved experiences. Instead of repeatedly talking through painful memories, EMDR allows the brain to reprocess them so they become integrated rather than overwhelming.

Many clients find that EMDR helps them move through experiences that have felt stuck for years.

What therapy looks like

Therapy is collaborative, thoughtful, and paced according to your needs. Our work may include:

  • assessing your strengths and protective factors
  • understanding your nervous system and stress patterns
  • identifying experiences that shape current responses and beliefs about self
  • EMDR therapy with bilateral stimulus (BLS) to process unresolved trauma
  • building practical regulation tools
  • developing a greater sense of safety and stability

Bilateral stimulation (BLS)

Bilateral stimulation (BLS) is a therapeutic technique involving rhythmic, alternating stimulation of the brain's left and right hemispheres, primarily used in EMDR therapy to process trauma and reduce stress.

It engages the brain through visual (eye movements), auditory (tones), or tactile (tapping) methods, and is adapted in telehealth sessions so you can participate from home.
